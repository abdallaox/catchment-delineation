This tool performs automated watershed delineation from a Digital Elevation Model (DEM). It implements a standard hydrological processing chain — from raw elevation data to a fully attributed drainage network and subbasin map — entirely within the browser.

Designed for hydrologists, catchment modellers, and flood risk analysts, it supports rapid basin characterisation without requiring GIS desktop software.

Primary outputs

  • Catchment boundary — the total drainage area contributing to your chosen outlet
  • River network — extracted stream lines attributed with Strahler order and flow accumulation
  • Subbasins — nested drainage units defined by major tributary confluences

All results are exportable as GeoPackage (for QGIS) or Shapefile.

1
DEM Conditioning pysheds
Raw DEMs contain artefact sinks — cells lower than all neighbours due to interpolation errors or data voids. These break flow routing by creating closed loops. Three conditioning steps are applied in sequence:
  • Pit filling — raises isolated single-cell sinks to the level of their lowest neighbour, enabling flow to escape.
  • Depression filling (Wang & Liu 2006) — floods closed multi-cell depressions to their spill point, ensuring all flow eventually reaches the domain boundary.
  • Flat resolution (Barnes et al. 2014) — adds a tiny epsilon gradient across flat areas (e.g. filled lake beds) so D8 routing produces a unique downstream direction rather than stalling.
2
D8 Flow Direction
Each conditioned cell is assigned to drain to the steepest of its 8 neighbours (O'Callaghan & Mark 1984). Direction is encoded as a power-of-2 integer (64=N, 128=NE, 1=E … 32=NW). This produces a single-flow-direction raster that defines the drainage network topology.
3
Flow Accumulation
For every cell, counts the total number of upstream cells that drain through it. High-accumulation cells correspond to rivers and valley bottoms. The maximum value equals the total number of DEM cells and occurs at the main outlet of the domain.
4
Outlet Snapping
When you click on the map, the tool automatically snaps to the highest-accumulation cell within a 15-pixel search radius that exceeds the accumulation threshold. This corrects for clicks that miss the stream centreline and ensures the delineation captures the correct drainage area.
5
Catchment Delineation BFS
A breadth-first search (BFS) traces all cells whose D8 flow path leads to the snapped outlet. For each cell in the queue, all 8 neighbours are tested — if a neighbour's flow direction points toward the current cell, it is added to the catchment. This is more reliable than pysheds' native grid.catchment() which can fail for boundary outlets.
6
Stream Extraction
Cells within the catchment where flow accumulation exceeds the Accumulation Threshold are classified as stream cells. The threshold controls the channel initiation area — the minimum drainage area required to sustain a permanent channel.
7
Strahler Stream Order
Stream cells are ordered using Strahler's classification (1957). Cells are processed in order of increasing accumulation (headwaters first):
  • Headwater cells (no upstream stream neighbours) → Order 1
  • Confluence of two order-n streams → Order n+1
  • Confluence where orders differ → Order = max of tributaries
8
River Network Vectorisation
The stream raster is converted to a topologically connected polyline network. Network nodes (headwaters, confluences, outlet) are identified. One polyline segment is traced downstream from each node to the next node, so all segments share exact endpoints — forming a valid connected network for GIS analysis and routing.
9
Subbasin Delineation
Pour points are placed at confluences where the Strahler order increases and both tributaries meet the Min Strahler Order threshold. BFS upstream traces are run from each pour point, processed upstream-first (sorted by accumulation) to prevent overlap. Any residual unassigned cells within the catchment are assigned to the nearest subbasin using a Euclidean distance transform.

Accumulation Threshold

The channel initiation threshold — the minimum number of upstream cells required for a cell to be classified as a stream. Conceptually equivalent to the minimum contributing area (km²) needed to initiate and sustain a channel.

ValueTypical interpretation
50–200Dense network including ephemeral & intermittent channels. Use for detailed hillslope analysis.
200–1000Perennial stream network. Good starting point for catchment modelling.
1000–5000Major rivers and main tributaries only. Use for large-scale basin studies.

Tip: For a 30m DEM, 500 cells ≈ 0.45 km² contributing area. For a 10m DEM, 500 cells ≈ 0.05 km². Always validate extracted channels against OS/EA river layers.

Min Strahler Order (subbasin splits)

Controls which confluences become subbasin pour points. Only junctions where both incoming tributaries have Strahler order ≥ this value will create a subbasin boundary.

ValueEffect
1Every headwater confluence creates a subbasin. Can produce dozens of tiny basins.
2Only splits at junctions of proper tributaries. Recommended default.
3Splits only at major tributary junctions — fewer, larger subbasins.
4+Only the highest-order confluences. Suitable for large catchments with high-order streams.

Tip: Start at 2 and increase if you get too many subbasins. The Max Strahler shown in results indicates what values are meaningful for your catchment.

Min Basin Size (% of catchment)

A minimum area filter that removes subbasins smaller than this percentage of the total catchment. Small basins typically arise from:

  • Short stream segments between closely-spaced confluences
  • DEM artefacts that create false bifurcations
  • Genuine small tributary catchments (if you want these, lower the value)

Tip: 2–5% is a good balance. If you see many thin slivers, increase to 10%. If you need fine-scale basin characterisation, drop to 1%.

Catchment Boundary

The dashed blue outline shows the total drainage area contributing to your outlet. Every cell inside will eventually route flow to the outlet under the D8 model. This boundary is a fundamental unit for:

  • Rainfall-runoff modelling (e.g. HEC-HMS, SWMM)
  • Flood frequency analysis
  • Water resource accounting
  • Pollutant source attribution

River Network & Strahler Order

Streams are coloured by order — light blue (order 1) → dark blue (higher orders). Line weight also increases with order. Use the order toggles to inspect individual orders.

Key properties of Strahler order:

  • Correlated with mean annual discharge and bankfull width
  • Each order increase ≈ 3–5× increase in drainage area (Horton's laws)
  • Order 1–2 streams dominate hydrograph response time
  • Order 3+ streams control sediment routing and in-channel processes

Subbasins

Each coloured polygon is an independent drainage unit with its own pour point at the downstream confluence. These are suitable as HRUs (Hydrological Response Units) for:

  • Distributed rainfall-runoff modelling
  • Flood routing (Muskingum-Cunge, etc.)
  • Sediment and nutrient budget calculations
  • Spatial prioritisation of flood mitigation measures

Hover over a subbasin to see its ID and cell count. The subbasin ID is preserved in the exported file.

D8 Single-Flow Direction

D8 routes all flow from a cell to a single downstream neighbour. This causes:

  • Parallel flow stripes on gentle slopes — flow is funnelled into artificial channels aligned with cardinal/diagonal directions
  • Underestimation of contributing area in flat or braided systems

Multiple-flow algorithms (MFD, D-infinity) address this but are computationally heavier and not yet implemented here.

DEM Resolution Effects

Stream network density is strongly resolution-dependent. A coarser DEM will miss smaller tributaries and produce a simpler network even at low thresholds. The DEM used here has a cell size of ~0.000278° (~25m at this latitude).

  • At 25m resolution, channels <50m wide may not be resolved
  • Urban areas with drainage infrastructure are not represented
  • Culverted watercourses can appear as disconnected

Depression Filling Artefacts

Genuine enclosed basins (lakes, reservoirs, karst) are treated as artefacts and filled. If your catchment contains lakes, the delineated boundary may differ from the true hydrological boundary.

Outlet Placement

Place your outlet at or very near a visible stream channel. Clicking on a hillslope will snap to the nearest stream cell, which may not be the intended location. The snapping radius is 15 cells — if no stream cell exists within that radius (given the current threshold), the click location is used as-is and may produce a very small or empty catchment.

Best practice: Zoom in before clicking; use a lower threshold if the stream network looks sparse at your outlet location.

Flat and Coastal Areas

Coastal floodplains and very flat terrain produce unreliable flow directions after DEM conditioning. Multiple valid paths may exist. Treat results in these areas with caution and validate against surveyed channel data.
